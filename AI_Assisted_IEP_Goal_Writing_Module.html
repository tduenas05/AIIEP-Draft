Section 1 · Why AI, Why Now

Why IEP Goal Writing Needs Support

  • Co-teaching teams often have 15 minutes or less of shared planning time to draft a goal.
  • Goals written under time pressure tend toward vague language — "will improve reading skills" — that's hard to measure and hard to defend at a review meeting.
  • AI drafting tools can speed up the first draft, but only if the team knows how to prompt them well and how to check what comes back.

What AI Can (and Can't) Do

${ICON_CHECK}

AI Can

  • Turn rough notes into a structured goal draft in seconds
  • Suggest measurable language and benchmark formats
  • Generate a few variations so the team can compare options
${ICON_X}

AI Can't

  • Know your student's actual present levels of performance
  • Guarantee legal compliance with IDEA or your state's code
  • Replace the co-teaching team's professional judgment

Section 2 · Anatomy of a SMART Goal

The Five Parts of a SMART IEP Goal

S
Specific

Names the skill and the condition — e.g., "during a 3-paragraph cold read"

M
Measurable

Includes a number and a way to track it — e.g., "8 of 10 trials"

A
Achievable

Realistic given the student's current baseline, not an aspirational guess

R
Relevant

Tied to the student's actual, documented area of need

T
Time-bound

States when progress will be checked — e.g., "by the next annual review"

Legal Guidelines · Before You Continue

Why This Is a Legal Issue, Not Just a Style Choice

  • IDEA requires every goal to be individualized to the actual student — not generic, not boilerplate, and not pulled unedited from a goal bank.
  • Recent research on AI-assisted IEPs found that tools generating full goals from very little student-specific information likely don't meet that individualization requirement unless the team substantially edits the output (Center for Democracy & Technology, 2025).
  • Policy is moving fast and varies by state. Georgia's 2025 state guidance, for example, already flags AI-written IEP goals as too high-risk for the technology's current reliability.
Know before you draft: check your state and district's current AI policy before you start. This module can walk you through good practice, but it can't tell you what your specific policy allows.
Legal Guidelines

AI Can Assist. AI Cannot Decide.

  • The IEP team — not a tool — is legally and ethically responsible for every goal. That responsibility can't be delegated to software.
  • Treat every AI draft as a first draft, never the final word: verify it against the student's real present-level data, then revise it as a team.
  • Never enter a student's name or other identifying details into an AI tool.
  • Tell families when AI was used to help draft a goal — ideally before the IEP meeting, not after.
This is exactly why the next section matters. The review workflow you're about to learn — verify data, check compliance, revise together, both partners sign off — is what keeps an AI-assisted goal legally defensible.

Sources: Center for Democracy & Technology (2025); Georgia Department of Education (2025); U.S. Department of Education (2025).

Before You Go

Key Takeaways

  • AI can speed up your first draft — it can't replace your knowledge of the student.
  • Every AI-drafted goal needs a human check against real data before it's final.
  • The 5-step workflow gives co-teaching teams a shared process, not just a shared document.
